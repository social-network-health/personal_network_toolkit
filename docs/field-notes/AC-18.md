# AC-18 — transports cannot read message contents · field notes

*Surfaced by the **Signal Desktop** evaluation (2026-06-27; Signal Desktop 8.16.0 @
`ffc75db55`) — the first time the evaluate flow ran against an app that is **not** a
PNA but **is** the canonical AC-18 transport. The AC:
[`spec/PNA_Spec.md` § Universal architectural commitments](../../spec/PNA_Spec.md#ac-18);
the axis pick that offers it: [`spec/axes.md` § Comms transport set](../../spec/axes.md#comms-transport-set)
(`comms:mailto-plus-signal`).*

## Lesson

AC-18 eligibility is a property of the **transport mechanism the workspace hands off
to**, judged **in isolation** — not of the downstream chain that handoff kicks off,
and not of whether the surrounding app is a good PNA. A transport passes if *its own
mechanism* cannot read message contents: encryption-in-protocol (Signal-class) passes;
`mailto:` passes (it hands the payload to whichever client the user configured, and the
toolkit's enforcement boundary ends at that handoff); a centralized broker that
**decodes the payload as part of operating** (Slack, Discord) fails *even if its
downstream looks "encrypted."*

Signal Desktop is the worked exemplar of the **content-blind-transport** property:
bodies and attachments are sealed with libsignal + sealed-sender *before* egress, so the
server sees only ciphertext (`ts/textsecure/OutgoingMessage.preload.ts:434-440`,
`:539-549`). The counterintuitive payoff is the decoupling it makes concrete: an app can
be the **ideal AC-18 transport while being non-conformant as a PNA**. Evaluated as a
candidate PNA, Signal fails the load-bearing commitments (an unsealed, SaaS-synced
private layer — AC-1, restated; background-syncs — AC-21), yet it is exactly the transport
the `comms:mailto-plus-signal` pick names. AC-18 conformance and PNA membership are
orthogonal; reading one off the other is the mistake.

## Negative invariants (what a candidate is easy to pass by accident)

- **A mechanism that reads/decodes the payload as part of operating MUST NOT be offered
  as a PNA-eligible transport — even if its downstream is "encrypted."** AC-18 names
  Slack/Discord (centralized message-broker SaaS that decodes payloads) as the failing
  class explicitly. *(Pinned by the AC-18 normative text; no reference-design negative
  test yet — `fellows_local_db`'s comms is `mailto-only`, so a `mailto-plus-signal`
  design is the one that would add `CO-3`'s negative test. Flagged honestly.)*
- **"The downstream provider encrypts it" MUST NOT be credited as the transport
  passing.** Eligibility is the mechanism handed off to, not what happens after.
  `mailto:` passes because the boundary ends at the handoff; the Gmail/Outlook behavior
  downstream is out of scope *by rule*, not by being trusted.
- **AC-18 conformance MUST NOT leak into a PNA-membership verdict.** An app being a
  perfect transport does not make it a PNA (Signal: AC-18 ✓, `pna-active` ✗). Keep the
  transport-eligibility finding off the top-line posture.
- **Content-blindness is NOT a claim about metadata.** AC-18 is satisfied by the
  mechanism not reading *contents*; it promises nothing about who-talks-to-whom or when.
  Contact-graph retention by the transport is explicitly *out of* the rule (too hard to
  enforce uniformly; varies by threat model) — don't fail a transport for metadata
  retention under AC-18, and don't over-credit an E2EE transport as "fully private."

## Pitfalls / look hardest at

- **The boundary is the *mechanism*, not the product.** Ask where the workspace hands
  the payload off and whether *that* thing can read content: `mailto:` → user's client
  (no); Signal protocol → sealed before egress (no); a Slack/Discord bot token → the
  broker decodes it (yes). Judge the handoff target, not the brand.
- **Don't run AC-16 against a thing that *is* a transport.** A single-transport
  messaging app (Signal, a bare mail client) has no transport *menu*, so AC-16
  (user-driven selection across multiple transports) is **not-applicable** to it — AC-16
  governs a PNA *workspace offering* transports, a different evaluation target than the
  transport itself.
- **Distribution-mechanism transports are not CO-3/AC-18 transports.** A distribution
  flavor's auth-link sender (e.g. Postmark for a magic-link install) is governed by
  Distribution-slot contracts (`CO-6`), not by the comms eligibility rule. Don't fold an
  auth-email sender into the AC-18 check.
- **E2EE ≠ AC-18 automatically — check the *send path*, not a dependency manifest.**
  Presence of a crypto library proves nothing; confirm the content is encrypted on the
  outbound path *before* it reaches the transport/server (for Signal, `signalEncrypt`
  then `sealedSenderEncrypt` ahead of the envelope).
